What is the story behind Mardi Gras?

Mardi Gras refers to events of the Carnival celebration, beginning on or after the Christian feasts of the Epiphany and culminating on the day before Ash Wednesday, which is known as Shrove Tuesday. Mardi Gras is French for "Fat Tuesday", reflecting the practice of the last night of eating rich, fatty foods before the ritual Lenten sacrifices and fasting of the Lenten season.

The origin of the holiday is the carnival celebration. The carnival is celebrated as a created event, but the roots of the carnival can be traced even before Christianity in the Greek celebrations in honor of Dionysus and in the Roman celebrations in honor of Saturnalia. The word "carnival" means "removing the meat" or "farewell from the meat" (from Latin: carni = meat, vale = farewell). Along with the practice of Catholic Christians to abstain from meat and all other bodily pleasures 40 days or six weeks before Easter, as an identification with the suffering of Jesus, a period known as 'Lent'. Before the meat there were feasts of gluttony and sins, which in Italy, Switzerland, France, Portugal and Spain were accompanied by street parties of dances, costumes and masks.

The holiday was brought to New Orleans by the French Catholic founders as a religious holiday. The holiday soon became a non-religious cultural feature, and is mainly celebrated in the tourist area of ​​the city's French Quarter. During the holiday, the streets of New Orleans are filled for about two weeks with unrestrained celebrations of dancing, marching and drunkenness, with costumes and especially colorful clothes, and sometimes with partial clothing of the participants in the festival[2].
